GPS vs Connected GPS: What's the Difference?

When shopping for a fitness tracker, you'll see "Built-in GPS" and "Connected GPS" listed in the specs. Here's what they mean and why it matters.

Built-in GPS

Built-in GPS means the tracker has its own GPS chip. It communicates directly with satellites to track your route, pace, and distance — no phone required. This is ideal for runners, cyclists, and hikers who want accurate tracking without carrying their phone.

Pros: More accurate, independent of phone, works anywhere

Cons: Uses more battery, adds cost to the device

Connected GPS

Connected GPS uses your smartphone's GPS. The tracker communicates with your phone via Bluetooth and uses its location data to map your route. This is common in budget fitness bands.

Pros: Lower cost, less battery drain on the tracker

Cons: Requires carrying your phone, less accurate in areas with poor phone reception

Which Should You Choose?

If you run or cycle without your phone, built-in GPS is essential. Models like the Garmin Venu 3, Polar Pacer Pro, and Xiaomi Smart Band 8 Pro all have built-in GPS.

If you always have your phone with you and just want basic activity tracking, connected GPS is fine. The Fitbit Charge 6 and Samsung Galaxy Fit3 use connected GPS and work well for most casual users.

The Bottom Line

  • Built-in GPS = no phone needed, more accurate, sips battery
  • Connected GPS = uses phone, OK for casual walkers
  • For serious runners/cyclists, built-in GPS is non-negotiable
  • Check battery life estimates — GPS use cuts it significantly
